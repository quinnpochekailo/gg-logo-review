Logo Explorations — April 2026

Three directions, exploring form only. All work is black on white — color comes later, once the structure is locked.

Target energy: Private membership clubs, modern luxury brands. The logo should whisper you're in the right room — not "nonprofit" or "finance." Think Soho House, Aesop, Celine — not Goldman Sachs.
Direction 1

The Seal

A modern emblem — debossed on heavy stock, embroidered on a lapel.
A refined emblem — not old-world heraldry, but a contemporary take on the wax seal or membership badge. Geometric abstraction, no literal imagery. This direction carries the most inherent authority and works well for physical applications: letterpress, embossing, lapel pins, event signage. It says institution without saying bank.
1A Diamond seal — text ring with geometric center
1B Modern shield — chevron + GG, minimal heraldry
1C Compass seal — cardinal ticks, GG center
1D Octagonal seal — faceted, classic authority
1E Wax ring — concentric circles, full wordmark
1F Black badge — solid circle, knockout GG
Direction 2

The Frame

A curated cartouche — the frame itself becomes the icon.
A distinctive border element that frames the wordmark. The frame can be extracted as a standalone mark. It suggests selected, curated, presented to you. Think of how high-end auction houses and galleries use framing devices. The most editorial direction — the frame works around anything you want to present as "chosen."
2A Editorial brackets — typographic brackets flank the name
2B Cornerstone frame — thin border, solid corner squares
2C Capsule cartouche — pill-shaped double border
2D Arch frame — architectural doorway motif
2E Pillar frame — vertical bars + horizontal rules
2F Cut-corner badge — octagonal frame, standalone icon
Direction 3

The Type-First Wordmark

No icon. The name IS the logo. "We don't need to explain ourselves."
The most restrained direction, and the hardest to get wrong. It ages the best, scales the cleanest, and telegraphs the most confidence. Each variant below references a specific typographic lineage used by real prestige brands. The final typeface would be custom-selected or licensed — these are close analogues via Google Fonts to show the feel.
3A — Bodoni High-contrast Didone. The fashion serif — Valentino, Vogue, Harper's Bazaar. Dramatic thin/thick stroke contrast. Signals editorial authority.
3B — Bodoni Lowercase Same high contrast, intimate register. Closer to Aesop, Byredo — luxury that doesn't announce itself.
3C — Playfair Display Transitional serif with Didone flair. Title case feels approachable but elevated. Think high-end hospitality — The Ned, Soho House print materials.
3D — Playfair + Raleway Heavy serif over featherweight sans. The contrast creates hierarchy — "grounded" anchors, "giving" floats. Tom Ford energy.
3E — Garamond The original prestige serif. Used by every Ivy League, every old-money institution. Celine's rebrand used a custom Garamond. Timeless, never trendy.
3F — Garamond Ruled Stacked with a hairline rule divider. Gallery / auction house energy — Christie's, Gagosian. The rule adds structure without weight.
3G — Cormorant Light Ultra-light Garamond variant. More delicate than EB Garamond — closer to the refined thinness of Cartier or Diptyque. Whisper-quiet.
3H — Cinzel Roman inscriptional capitals. Trajan's Column lineage — Bulgari, Tiffany & Co., The Ritz. Pure classical authority. Caps only by design.
3I — Cinzel + Jost Classical capitals over geometric sans. Old authority meets modern restraint. The pairing says institution with taste.
3J — Crimson Pro Warm old-style serif with generous proportions. Less austere than Garamond — closer to the approachable sophistication of J.Crew or Restoration Hardware.
3K — Baskerville The British serif. Used by the Government of Canada, Kate Spade, many law firms. Balanced, trustworthy, slightly warmer than Garamond. Quiet authority.
3L — Source Serif Adobe's modern serif — clean, slightly technical, extremely legible. The "tech company that reads books" feel. Stripe's typographic neighborhood.
3M — Prata High-contrast display serif, Didot-adjacent. More personality than Bodoni — slightly editorial, slightly fashion. Departures magazine energy.
3N — Jost Futura's modern cousin. Supreme, Louis Vuitton, Dolce & Gabbana all use Futura variants. Geometric perfection — every letter built from circles and lines.
3O — Jost Thin Same geometry, hairline weight. Calvin Klein, COS, Helmut Lang territory. The thinnest stroke that still reads — pure restraint.
3P — Raleway Light Elegant geometric sans with distinctive single-story 'a'. Light weight + wide tracking = the Bottega Veneta / The Row approach. Expensive silence.
3Q — Montserrat Geometric sans inspired by Buenos Aires signage. Warmer than Futura, more character than Helvetica. Widely used in luxury hospitality and real estate.
3R — DM Sans Clean low-contrast geometric. The modern tech-luxury crossover — Notion, Linear, Vercel. Contemporary without trying. Reads as "built this decade."
3S — Inter Medium The default of modern SaaS — but medium weight + heavy tracking elevates it. GitHub, Figma, Raycast. Clean, confident, zero pretension.
3T — Bodoni + DM Sans Fashion serif over tech sans. High drama meets cool restraint. The pairing a private bank would use if it were founded in 2025.
